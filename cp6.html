7  Need, Lack, and Dependency

Need is not weakness.

Before need becomes shame, dependency, compensation, substitution, or repetition, it begins as a condition of existence. A person needs food, shelter, rest, safety, touch, recognition, belonging, agency, meaning, and love. These are not luxuries. They are conditions under which a self can survive, develop, and function.

But need has become complicated in many people. To need is to depend. To depend is to risk disappointment, rejection, or control. To express need is to make vulnerability visible. To feel need without response is to experience lack. To live with unresolved need is to organize life around its compensation.

Self-understanding must therefore include need. The question is not whether a person has needs. Everyone does. The question is: which needs are unmet? How does the self cope with unmet need? What has need become in the self—shame, demand, denial, substitution, repetition, or honest acknowledgment?

This chapter explores need as structural condition, lack as evidence of what was missing, dependency as fundamental reality, and the path through need toward greater self-knowledge. Need, like desire and fear, is not something to eliminate. It is something to understand.

7.1 Need as Condition

This section establishes need as a foundational condition of existence rather than a flaw. It explores how need precedes desire and fear, and how acknowledging need honestly is essential for self-understanding.

Need is not desire. Desire moves toward something experienced as valuable. Need requires something for continued existence or development. A person may desire recognition. They need attachment. A person may desire wealth. They may need safety. A person may desire achievement. They may need competence and agency. A person may desire love. They need belonging.

Desire points toward value. Need points toward condition. Need says: without this, something in the self cannot live, develop, function, or become. To be a self is to be conditioned. A self does not exist in pure independence. It exists within a body that requires care, within a social world that requires recognition, within an environment that requires support. Need is the name for these conditions.

When a person does not acknowledge their needs, the needs do not disappear. They become expressed indirectly through symptoms, compensation, demand, avoidance, or dysfunction. A person may deny needing connection, but become depressed, lonely, or desperate. They may deny needing agency, but become resentful in dependent positions. They may deny needing recognition, but burn out from unrecognized effort. They may deny needing safety, but become anxious in uncertain environments.

The denial of need does not eliminate the need. It eliminates the ability to recognize, name, and respond to the condition. Self-understanding begins with: What do I need? Not what should I need. Not what would impress others. Not what would avoid shame. What, in this situation, does my existence require?

This question is not always answered immediately. Many people have been taught that need is weakness. They have learned to suppress, ignore, or override their needs. To ask “What do I need?” may feel unfamiliar, vulnerable, or dangerous. But the question must be asked repeatedly.

The following questions support this inquiry: What does my body need right now—rest, movement, food, air, warmth, touch, safety, medical attention? What does my emotional system need—connection, recognition, space, time, comfort, expression, validation, confrontation? What does my developmental process need—challenge, support, feedback, resources, teaching, practice, exposure? What does my social self need—belonging, role, contribution, respect, fairness, community? What does my structural becoming need—direction, skill, stamina, support, reality testing, value alignment, conditions, capability?

Need, properly understood, is not a request for rescue. It is information about what conditions are required for the self to continue developing. A responsible self attends to its own conditions.

7.2 Lack as Evidence and Dependency as Reality

This section explores lack as the felt experience of unmet need, and dependency as the fundamental reality of human existence. Both challenge the fantasy of complete independence.

Lack is the subjective experience of need not being met. When a need is unmet, the self may feel absence, longing, pain, incompleteness, emptiness, hunger, or deficiency. Lack is not the same as need. Need is structural. Lack is experiential. A person may need something and not experience lack if the need is met. A person may experience lack and not know what need it points toward.

Lack matters for self-understanding because it provides evidence. Chronic feelings of emptiness may point toward unmet need for meaning, connection, recognition, or purpose. A persistent sense of inadequacy may point toward unmet need for competence, mastery, or social value. A recurring hunger for attention may point toward unmet need for being seen and accepted. A constant dissatisfaction with achievement may point toward unmet need for authentic recognition rather than performance.

Lack asks: What was missing? What has not been provided? What have I not received that I needed? This question is not about blame. It is about understanding the structure of the self’s history. A person’s developmental environment may have been unable to provide certain conditions. This creates lack. Lack becomes evidence of what was insufficiently present.

The goal is not to remain in lack. The goal is to understand what has been absent so the self can now respond differently. Lack can motivate healthy seeking. It can also create compulsive patterns if the origin is not understood. Lack says: something was not there. Now the self must decide what to do with this knowledge.

Dependency is the corollary of need. To need something is to be dependent on something outside the self for its provision. The infant depends on the caregiver. The child depends on the family. The student depends on the teacher. The worker depends on the employer. The body depends on food, water, air, and shelter. The self depends on relationship for recognition, belonging, and love.

Dependency is not a failure of becoming. It is a condition of existence. No self is fully independent. Complete autonomy is not achievable. A healthy self is not one that needs nothing. A healthy self is one that can recognize its dependencies, choose them responsibly, build structures that meet its needs, and tolerate necessary dependence.

However, dependency can become problematic when it is denied, when it is excessive, when it is avoidable but not addressed, or when it is exploitative. The difference is not between dependence and independence. The difference is between responsible dependence and trapped dependence.

Responsible dependence says: I need support in this area. I will seek it responsibly. I will receive it without shame. I will reciprocate where possible. I will build my capability over time.

Trapped dependence says: I cannot survive without this specific person or system. I have no alternatives. I am afraid of losing this support. I will do anything to maintain it.

Self-understanding asks: What am I dependent on? Is this dependence necessary, chosen, or avoidable? Can I build more capacity without denying my need? Am I trapped or supported?

7.3 Denied Need, Shame, and Entitlement

This section examines what happens when need is denied, when shame surrounds having needs, and when need becomes distorted into entitlement. These patterns reveal the inner relationship a person has with their own condition.

Denied need does not disappear. It becomes expressed through substitutes, symptoms, demands, or dysfunction. A person who denies needing rest may push through exhaustion and eventually collapse. A person who denies needing connection may become depressed or numb. A person who denies needing recognition may dismiss others who want recognition, but remain unsatisfied. A person who denies needing support may refuse help until crisis forces them to accept.

Denial of need often originates in an environment where need was not safely met. The child may have learned that expressing need leads to rejection, punishment, or disappointment. The child may have concluded: My needs will not be met. It hurts less not to need. I will survive by needing nothing.

This conclusion may have been adaptive in the original environment. But in a different context, the same pattern becomes limiting. The person may reject support, avoid asking for help, refuse rest, and treat all need as weakness. This is not true strength. It is unprocessed adaptation.

The path forward is not to suddenly surrender all boundaries. It is to gradually re-learn that some needs can be acknowledged and met in safe, appropriate ways. The self must ask: What need am I pretending does not exist? What would it mean to admit this need to myself? What risk do I associate with need? What would be required to allow myself to need, responsibly?

Shame is often attached to need. The self may feel: I should not need this. I should be able to handle this. Needing this proves I am weak, immature, burdensome, or defective.

Shame around need often comes from family, culture, or personal history. Some families communicate that need is weakness, inconvenience, or failure. Some cultures reward self-sufficiency and treat dependency as failure. Some personal experiences of need being rejected, ignored, or punished create lasting shame.

Shame prevents honest recognition of need. When shame is active, the self may not admit needing help, support, rest, connection, recognition, or love even to itself. Shame drives need underground. Underground need does not disappear. It creates indirect patterns: compensatory behavior, passive demand, hidden dependence, or chronic dissatisfaction.

Self-understanding asks: What need feels shameful to me? What story did I internalize about this need? What happened when I expressed this need earlier in my life? What would help me hold this need with less shame? Can I recognize that needing does not make me defective? That having needs is part of being a person?

Shame is not solved by declaring it gone. It is reduced by honest recognition, compassionate self-awareness, and real experiences of need being met respectfully.

Shame can also produce its opposite: entitlement. A person whose needs were met inconsistently or conditionally may develop a demand pattern. They may feel: I should not have to ask. Others should see what I need. If I have to ask, it does not count. I am entitled to having my needs met without communication. Others who do not meet my needs are failing me.

Entitlement is an attempt to solve need without vulnerability. It demands provision without exposure. It insists on care without communication. It avoids the vulnerability of asking by turning need into expectation.

Entitlement creates relationship problems, unmet expectations, and ongoing dissatisfaction. The person receives care, but it never feels like enough because it was not freely offered. The deeper structure is often: I still need to feel that someone wants to meet my need. I need my need to be met without me having to risk rejection.

Self-understanding asks: Do I express need as request or demand? Can I tolerate the vulnerability of asking? What happens inside me when I have to state my need? Can I risk receiving care that was asked for?

7.4 Need, Agency, and Independence

This section explores the relationship between need and personal agency, challenging the myth of complete independence. Need does not eliminate agency, and acknowledging dependence can actually deepen responsible action.

Need does not cancel agency. Even when a person needs something, they can act. The presence of need does not make the person passive. A person who needs medical care can seek it, choose providers, and follow treatment. A person who needs recognition can communicate, demonstrate work, and negotiate visibility. A person who needs belonging can choose groups, contribute value, and build connection. A person who needs meaning can explore values, create project, and engage in making sense of life.

The distinction is between passive need and active need. Passive need says: I need it. Someone else should provide it. Active need says: I need it. What responsible action is available to me? Some needs cannot be met by the self alone. Dependency is real. But even within dependency, agency exists. A person can choose how to seek support, how to communicate need, how to build relationships that meet reciprocal needs, how to build capacity over time, and how to respond when need is not met.

Self-understanding asks: What can I do about my needs? Not everything. But something. Where can I act? Where do I need to receive? Where can I build capability? Where do I need to seek help? Need and agency are not opposites. Agency within acknowledged need is more mature than pretending no needs exist.

Independence is not the absence of need. Complete independence is a fantasy. A self does not exist without body, without history, without society, without environment, without other selves. Everyone depends on food, shelter, air, and other humans. The fantasy of total independence is often a reaction to shame about dependency. It may be an attempt to avoid vulnerability, control, or disappointment.

True maturity is not independence in the sense of needing nothing. It is the capacity to recognize and manage one’s dependencies responsibly. A person with mature independence can ask for help when needed. They can set boundaries as needed. They can receive care without shame. They can give care without resentment. They can be alone without loneliness. They can be in relationship without losing themselves. They can accept that some needs must be met through others and that this does not make them less of a self.

Self-understanding asks: Am I pretending to be independent when I am not? Where do I need support but resist asking? Where would acknowledging dependency make me more effective? What would responsible dependence look like for me?

7.5 Need in Relationship, Development, and the Body

This section examines need as it manifests in relationships, throughout development, and in the body. These three domains reveal how pervasive need is in human existence.

Need shapes relationship. Every relationship involves exchange of need. A relationship may meet needs for connection, recognition, safety, touch, companionship, validation, support, challenge, growth, shared meaning, play, and care. When needs are met reciprocally and communicated honestly, relationships can support becoming. When needs are unacknowledged, mismatched, or suppressed, relationships become distorted.

Some relationship problems are actually need problems. A person who feels chronically unloved may have unmet need for consistent affection, attention, or attunement. A person who repeatedly feels controlled may have unmet need for autonomy and agency. A person who feels invisible may have unmet need for recognition. A person who feels used may have unmet need for mutual care. A person who feels suffocated may have unmet need for space and boundaries.

Relationship need is not a sign of failure. It is evidence that relationships exist to meet real conditions. A mature relationship is not one where no needs exist. It is one where needs can be communicated, negotiated, met to a reasonable degree, and held with compassion.

The self must ask: What do I need from my relationships? What do I need that I have not communicated? What do I expect without expressing? What do I fear losing? What need do I demand without vulnerability? What need do I suppress without awareness?

Need also changes across development. An infant needs safety, nourishment, attachment, soothing. A child needs structure, recognition, exploration, limit. An adolescent needs identity, autonomy, peer connection, meaningful challenge. A young adult needs direction, competence, contribution, intimacy. A mature adult needs meaning, integration, purpose, generativity, legacy.

As a person develops, their needs become more complex, internal, and differentiated. But no stage of development is without need. The form changes. The reality remains.

This is relevant because some people judge themselves for having needs at a later stage. They may think: By now I should not need approval, recognition, support, or guidance. But development does not eliminate need. It transforms need.

Self-understanding asks: What does my current developmental stage require? Am I holding needs appropriate to my age, stage, and context? Am I confusing need with immaturity? What needs are emerging as I grow? What needs am I outgrowing?

The body is a fundamental site of need. The body needs air, water, food, sleep, movement, safety, touch, warmth, rest, and care. When these needs are unmet, the body signals through discomfort, tension, fatigue, pain, illness, restlessness, or collapse.

Many people have learned to override the body’s need signals. They push through fatigue, ignore hunger, suppress pain, avoid rest, and neglect their physical conditions. This is not strength. It is dissociation from the conditions of existence.

Self-understanding includes listening to the body. The body asks: Have I rested enough? Have I eaten enough? Have I moved enough? Have I been touched enough? Have I been safe enough? Do I need to stop? Do I need help? Do I need to leave? Do I need care?

The body is not the only source of truth. The body can also carry trauma, habit, and conditioned response. But the body is a valid channel of information about need. A self-model that ignores the body’s needs is built on denial. Self-understanding attends to the body’s signals with respect and discernment.

7.6 Need, Substitution, and Reality

This section explores how unmet need often leads to substitution and repetition, and how need must eventually be brought into contact with reality. Reading need structurally becomes essential for interrupting these patterns.

When a primary need cannot be met, the self often seeks a substitute. A person who needs recognition but cannot get it may pursue money, status, or achievement. A person who needs but cannot find intimacy may pursue work, food, consumption, or entertainment. A person who needs meaning may pursue accumulation, travel, fitness, or compulsive achievement. A person who needs belonging may pursue social approval, conformity, or performance.

Substitution is not always bad. Some substitutes provide genuine benefit. A person may channel unmet need for recognition into productive achievement. A person may transform need for belonging into community contribution. A person may use career success to meet needs for meaning and competence.

But substitution becomes dangerous when it is not recognized as substitution. If a person believes they only need money, while deeper need for recognition or belonging remains unmet, accumulation may never satisfy. The surface need is met. The deeper need continues.

Substitution creates patterns of pursuing one thing while needing another. The result may be: achievement without fulfillment, accumulation without satisfaction, social approval without genuine connection, activity without meaning. The person may feel empty despite meeting all their stated goals.

Self-understanding asks: What need might be hiding behind my surface goals? What am I pursuing that I hope will finally make me feel whole? What have I substituted? What would happen if my deeper need were met? What would I have to risk to pursue the original need rather than the substitute?

Unresolved need also creates repetition. A person who did not receive adequate attunement in childhood may repeatedly seek relationships where they try to gain attunement from unavailable partners. A person who did not feel safe in their early environment may repeatedly seek safety in controlling situations. A person who was not recognized may repeatedly seek contexts where recognition is conditional or competitive.

Repetition is the attempt to resolve old need in a new environment. It is not pathology. It is the self trying to finish unfinished business. But repetition often fails because the new situation, chosen by unconscious pattern, may reproduce the old conditions.

The path forward is not to repeat endlessly, but to recognize the pattern, understand the original need, and find new ways to meet the need in present reality. This requires: pattern recognition, original need identification, grief for what was missing, awareness of current conditions, responsible action.

Need must also be tested by reality. Not every need can be met perfectly. Some needs from childhood can never be fully met by an adult self in the same way. The original environment cannot be recreated. The parent cannot become different. The past cannot be revised. This is not a comfortable truth, but it is a necessary one.

The question becomes: Given that some needs were not met and may never be fully met in the original way, what can the adult self do? The answer is not resignation. The answer includes acknowledgment: this was missing; mourning: I did not receive what I needed; adaptation: I can now meet this need in adult form or find partial satisfaction in new relationships and structures; agency: I can seek what is available; compassion: I can hold my need without shame; and integration: I can use this understanding to know myself.

Reading need structurally requires asking the following questions. What do I need? What have I received and what was insufficient? What need do I feel ashamed of? What need have I substituted? What pattern do I repeat? What old need is trying to be met in current situations? What is available now? What must I accept was not provided? What can I now provide for myself or responsibly seek from others? What would responsible action toward my need look like? And finally, what does my need teach me about my conditions?

7.7 From Need to Self-Knowledge

This concluding section summarizes the chapter’s key teachings about need, lack, and dependency. Need, properly understood, is not the enemy of becoming. It is one of its essential informants.

Need is condition. Lack is evidence of what was missing. Dependency is reality. Shame distorts need into hidden burden. Entitlement distorts need into demand. Agency within need is mature. Independence is not the absence of need, but the capacity to manage it responsibly. Substitution reveals what was unavailable. Repetition reveals what remains unresolved.

The path through need is not to eliminate it. The path is to recognize, acknowledge, and respond to need with greater honesty, agency, and self-compassion. A person who can say “I need” without shame, “I lacked” without blame, and “I depend” without collapse has gained significant self-knowledge.

The key teachings of this chapter are as follows. First, need is a structural condition of existence, not a weakness or flaw. Second, lack provides evidence of what was missing and points toward what may still be required. Third, dependency is a fundamental reality for all human beings and is not a failure. Fourth, denied need does not disappear but becomes expressed through indirect patterns. Fifth, shame and entitlement are two common distortions of need. Sixth, agency is possible within need, and true maturity is responsible dependence, not complete independence. Seventh, need manifests in relationships, across development, and through the body. Eighth, substitution and repetition are common responses to unmet need that must be recognized. Ninth, need must be tested by reality, including accepting what was not and may not be available. And tenth, reading need structurally transforms condition into self-knowledge.

Need is condition. Lack is evidence. Dependency is reality. Knowledge of all three is self-knowledge.

⚡ 核心知识 | Core Knowledge — 必须记住

  1. Need is a structural condition of existence, not a weakness — Everyone has needs. The question is: which needs are unmet, and how do I cope with that lack?
  2. Lack is evidence of unmet need — Chronic emptiness may point toward unmet needs for meaning, connection, recognition, or purpose. Lack is not a defect; it is information.
  3. Dependency is a fundamental human reality — Complete independence is a fantasy. Maturity is not needing nothing, but managing one’s dependencies responsibly.
  4. Denied needs do not disappear — They express themselves indirectly: compensation, symptoms, collapse, resentment, control. Denial does not eliminate the need — it eliminates the ability to recognize and respond to it.
  5. Shame and entitlement are two common distortions of need — Shame hides need; entitlement demands without vulnerability. Both block healthy expression of need.
  6. Agency exists within need — Even with dependencies, one can still act — in how one seeks support, communicates, and builds reciprocal relationships.
  7. Need manifests in relationships, development, and the body — Pay attention to bodily signals and how needs change across developmental stages.
