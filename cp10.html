11  Wound and Sensitivity

A wound is not only something that happened.

A wound is something that continues to organize perception, reaction, protection, and identity after the original injury has passed.

The event may be over.
The relationship may be gone.
The situation may have changed.
The person may have grown older, stronger, more capable, more intelligent, or more independent.

But the wound may still remain active.

It remains active when a present situation touches an old injury and the self reacts as if the old danger has returned. The present event may be small, but the reaction is large. The words may be mild, but the shame is intense. The silence may be temporary, but the fear feels like abandonment. The criticism may be specific, but the self feels condemned. The comparison may be ordinary, but the self feels erased.

This is sensitivity.

Sensitivity is not random weakness. It is often the self’s memory of injury.

A sensitive zone is a place where the self has learned:

This hurts.
This is dangerous.
This exposes me.
This makes me small.
This makes me unwanted.
This makes me invisible.
This makes me powerless.
This must not happen again.

Wound and sensitivity matter for self-understanding because they explain why some reactions are larger than the present moment seems to justify.

A person may not understand themselves if they only look at the surface event. They must ask what the event touched.

The central claim of this chapter is:

A wound is a past injury that continues to shape present sensitivity.

To understand wound is not to remain trapped in the past.

It is to understand how the past may still be operating inside the present.

11.1 Wound as Continuing Structure

A wound becomes self-structure when it continues beyond the original event. The original injury may be over, but the expectations, rules, and protective patterns it produced remain active, organizing how the self perceives and responds to the present. Sensitivity is the signal that such structure has been activated — the moment when a present event touches an old wound and the reaction exceeds what the surface situation alone would justify.

The original event may involve humiliation, abandonment, rejection, betrayal, neglect, failure, ridicule, exclusion, poverty, violence, control, invisibility, or lack of recognition. These events matter not only because they cause pain but because they teach the self what to expect. A humiliated self may expect exposure to become attack. An abandoned self may expect closeness to become loss. A rejected self may expect desire to become shame. A neglected self may expect need to be ignored. A betrayed self may expect trust to become danger. A controlled self may expect commitment to become imprisonment. An unseen self may expect achievement to be necessary for recognition.

The wound becomes a set of internal rules:

If I am visible, I may be humiliated.
If I need someone, I may be abandoned.
If I trust, I may be betrayed.
If I ask, I may be rejected.
If I fail, I may be worthless.
If I rest, I may be useless.
If I show weakness, I may be attacked.

These rules may not be stated consciously. They may appear as reaction, avoidance, tension, defensiveness, numbness, anger, perfectionism, or withdrawal. The wound is no longer only remembered — it is enacted. This is why self-understanding requires more than asking “What happened to me?” It must also ask: What structure did this injury leave behind? What situations now activate it? What interpretation does it produce? What protection does it demand? What future does it make difficult?

Sensitivity reveals where a wound may be active, often through disproportion — the reaction is larger than the event, the defense is stronger than the threat, the shame is deeper than the mistake, the fear is older than the present danger. Sensitivity says: something here matters more than it appears. For example, someone gives a small correction and the self feels humiliated — the correction may have touched an old wound around being wrong, stupid, exposed, or inferior. Someone does not reply to a message and the self feels abandoned — the silence may have touched an old wound around being ignored, forgotten, or not chosen. Someone succeeds publicly and the self feels erased — the success may have touched an old wound around invisibility, comparison, or unmet recognition.

A sensitive reaction should not be dismissed as irrational. It should be studied. The question is not only “Why am I reacting like this?” but “What old meaning has this present event activated?” Sensitivity is evidence. It points to an area of the self that needs structure, care, boundary, and new evidence.

Sensitivity is not a flaw to be eliminated but evidence to be studied. It points to the places where the self still carries the past in a way that organizes present experience. Reading sensitivity as signal rather than weakness is the first step toward understanding the wound beneath it.

11.2 Wounds of Visibility

Some of the deepest wounds concern how the self is seen — or not seen — by others. Humiliation, shame, and unmet recognition each involve a painful relation to visibility: the experience of being reduced by exposure, condemned by defect, or erased by invisibility. Though distinct in their structure, these three wounds share the fundamental challenge of needing to be seen without being harmed.

11.2.1 Humiliation and the Wound of Exposure

Humiliation is one of the most powerful sources of wound. It does not only hurt — it makes the self feel exposed under judgment. The person feels seen in the worst possible way, reduced to a mistake, weakness, lack, failure, body, accent, poverty, ignorance, awkwardness, or need. They do not merely think “I did something wrong.” They feel “I have been made small.”

Humiliation often creates a lasting fear of visibility. A person may become afraid to speak, publish, perform, ask questions, try publicly, show incomplete work, admit not knowing, or enter situations where they can be evaluated. The wound says: if I am seen, I can be reduced; if I try and fail, I will be exposed; if I show incompleteness, I will lose dignity. This can produce patterns including perfectionism, overpreparation, silence, avoidance of public attempts, defensiveness around feedback, fear of asking basic questions, refusal to begin as a beginner, and hiding work until it feels flawless. The self may call this “high standards,” but when standards exist mainly to prevent exposure, they become a wound defense.

Self-understanding asks: Where do I fear being seen before I am perfect? Where does visibility feel dangerous? What kind of mistake feels unbearable? What public situation makes me feel small? What humiliation am I trying never to repeat?

To heal this wound structurally, the self needs new forms of exposure that preserve dignity — not reckless exposure, not forced performance, but gradual exposure. A small question asked. A small piece of work shared. A first draft tolerated. A correction received without collapse. A beginner identity accepted. A mistake separated from total worth. The wound of humiliation is repaired when visibility no longer automatically means annihilation.

11.2.2 Shame and the Wound of Defectiveness

Shame is closely related to wound but has its own structure. Shame says: something is wrong with me. It turns experience into identity. A person may feel shame around need, weakness, desire, failure, body, background, poverty, family, education, emotional intensity, dependency, sexuality, ambition, anger, envy, or lack of achievement. Once shame attaches to an area, the self becomes sensitive there. A small comment can hurt deeply. A small comparison can create collapse. A small failure can become identity proof. A small exposure can trigger hiding.

Shame makes the self afraid of being known. It says: if they see this, they will know the truth about me; if they know the truth, I will be rejected. This produces secrecy. The person hides the area, performs the opposite, attacks others who touch it, or builds identity around not needing what they secretly need. Shame around need may become performed independence. Shame around ignorance may become performed intelligence. Shame around poverty may become status anxiety. Shame around failure may become perfectionism. Shame around desire may become indifference. Shame around weakness may become hardness.

The wound of shame does not heal through simple reassurance alone; the deeper work is structural. The self must learn to name the shame, separate evidence from identity, locate the wound, reduce secrecy, build tolerable exposure, receive non-destructive recognition, and act without waiting for perfect self-worth. The self must learn to say: this is an area of shame; it is not the whole of me; it has history; it shapes my reactions; it needs dignity, not denial. Shame loses some of its power when it becomes observable.

11.2.3 Unmet Recognition and the Wound of Invisibility

A person can be wounded not only by direct attack but by not being seen. Unmet recognition creates a different kind of sensitivity. The person may feel: no one noticed; no one understood; no one valued this; no one saw what it cost me; no one recognized what I could become; no one reflected my worth back to me. This can create a wound of invisibility.

The self begins to organize around being seen. It may chase achievement, status, beauty, excellence, influence, performance, or public validation. It may become hungry for recognition but ashamed of that hunger. It may feel intense envy when others are recognized, anger when contribution is ignored, or despair when effort remains unseen. Unmet recognition often produces a repeated question: Do I matter? This wound may appear as overachievement, recognition hunger, resentment when ignored, sensitivity to praise and criticism, envy of visible people, fear of being ordinary, difficulty working privately, or dependence on external validation.

The self should not condemn recognition need too quickly. Human beings need recognition — to be seen by others is part of becoming real in a social world. But unmet recognition becomes dangerous when it turns the whole life into a performance for an imagined audience. Self-understanding asks: Where do I feel unseen? Whose recognition did I need but not receive? What kind of recognition do I keep trying to obtain? What do I want others to finally understand about me? What do I stop doing when no one sees? What remains meaningful even without recognition?

The wound of invisibility requires a double movement. First, the self must acknowledge the need to be seen. Second, the self must build a life that is not entirely governed by being seen. Recognition should support identity — it should not own it.

The wounds of visibility all involve a broken relationship between the self and the eyes of others. Humiliation makes exposure feel like annihilation, shame makes being known feel like rejection, and unmet recognition makes existence feel unnoticed. Healing each requires not the elimination of visibility but the gradual construction of safe ways to be seen — experiences where being known does not lead to reduction.

11.3 Wounds of Relational Safety

Beyond how the self is seen, wounds also form around whether the self can feel safe in relationship with others. Rejection, abandonment, and betrayal each damage the capacity for trust, closeness, and dependence in distinct ways. These wounds teach the self that relational openness leads to harm, producing patterns of anxious pursuit, defensive withdrawal, or hypervigilant suspicion.

11.3.1 Rejection and the Wound of Being Unchosen

Rejection wounds the self when it becomes more than one person’s refusal — when it becomes a statement about worth. The person does not only feel “They did not choose me,” but “I am not chosen; I am not wanted; I am not desirable; I am not enough.” This wound often becomes active in relationships, applications, friendships, social groups, professional opportunities, and public evaluation.

It can create two opposite patterns. One pattern is pursuit: prove worth, seek approval, overgive, perform desirability, chase unavailable people, become dependent on signs of interest. Another pattern is avoidance: do not ask, do not apply, do not express desire, reject first, withdraw before being rejected, pretend not to care. Both patterns are organized around the wound. One says “I must become chosen.” The other says “I must never risk being unchosen again.”

Self-understanding asks: Where do I avoid asking because refusal would hurt too much? Where do I overperform to be chosen? Where do I choose unavailable people because the wound is familiar? Where do I confuse one rejection with total unworthiness? Where do I reject myself before others can reject me?

The work is to separate rejection from identity. A rejection may be real. It may hurt. It may contain useful feedback, reveal mismatch, or indicate lack of skill, timing, compatibility, or readiness. But it does not automatically define the whole self. The wound of being unchosen begins to heal when the self can survive refusal without collapsing into total unworthiness.

11.3.2 Abandonment and the Wound of Loss

Abandonment creates sensitivity around absence. A person wounded by abandonment may react strongly to distance, silence, delay, ambiguity, separation, or emotional unavailability. The present event may be small — a late reply, a canceled plan, a distracted tone, a change in someone’s attention, a temporary separation — but the self may experience it as danger. The wound says: they are leaving; I will be left alone; connection is unsafe; need leads to loss; do not depend too much; hold tightly or detach first.

This wound may produce anxious pursuit or defensive distance. Anxious pursuit says: I must secure the connection; I must check; I must ask again; I must become more lovable. Defensive distance says: I will not need them; I will leave first; I will stay detached; I will not let anyone matter too much. Both are attempts to manage abandonment fear.

Self-understanding asks: What kind of absence activates me? What story do I tell when someone is unavailable? Do I pursue, protest, control, withdraw, or numb? How old does this fear feel? What evidence belongs to the present and what belongs to the past?

The wound of abandonment requires the self to build a more stable relation to absence. Not every silence is abandonment. Not every distance is rejection. Not every delay means loss. Not every need must become panic. But the self also needs real relational conditions: reliability, communication, boundary, and trust. The goal is not to shame the fear but to update the pattern.

11.3.3 Betrayal and the Wound of Trust

Betrayal wounds the self’s ability to trust. When trust has been broken, the self may become hyper-alert to inconsistency, secrecy, ambiguity, or dependence. It may search for signs of danger, test people, withhold vulnerability, or assume hidden motives. It may confuse caution with wisdom and suspicion with intelligence. The wound says: if I trust, I can be harmed; if I relax, I will be deceived; if I depend, I will be used; if I reveal myself, it will be turned against me.

This wound can protect the self from real danger — trust should not be naive, some people are unsafe, some systems exploit openness, and some relationships do not deserve access. But when betrayal wound governs too broadly, it blocks intimacy, collaboration, mentorship, and repair. The self may become safe but alone.

Self-understanding asks: Where do I assume betrayal before evidence? Where do I test people instead of communicating? Where do I make others pay for someone else’s betrayal? Where is my caution realistic and where is it wound-driven? What kind of trust can be built gradually?

Trust after betrayal does not mean returning to innocence. It means developing discriminating trust. The self learns: not everyone deserves access, but not everyone is the betrayer; trust can be staged, bounded, and tested through behavior over time. The wound of betrayal requires boundary, evidence, and gradual reopening.

Rejection, abandonment, and betrayal each fracture a different dimension of relational safety — being chosen, being held, and being able to trust. Despite their differences, all three produce a central dilemma: the self needs connection but has learned that connection is dangerous. Healing requires the development of discriminating trust, gradual exposure to reliability, and the separation of old patterns from present evidence.

11.4 Wounds of Power and Position

Wounds also form around the self’s sense of power and standing relative to others. The wound of powerlessness makes autonomy feel urgent and surrender feel dangerous. The wound of inferiority makes comparison into a measure of worth. Both distort how the self navigates choice, hierarchy, and evaluation.

11.4.1 Control and the Wound of Powerlessness

Some wounds form around powerlessness. The person may have been controlled, trapped, overruled, dismissed, coerced, or unable to leave. Their preferences may not have mattered. Their no may not have been respected. Their environment may have taught them that agency was unsafe or useless.

Later, this wound may create strong sensitivity around control. The self may react intensely to authority, obligation, commitment, dependency, rules, expectations, or requests. It may say: I will not be trapped again; no one will control me; I must keep exit power; I must not owe anyone; I must decide everything myself. This can produce fierce independence, but it can also produce fear of commitment, difficulty cooperating, resistance to structure, or inability to receive guidance.

The wound of powerlessness often creates an overcorrection toward control. A person who once had no control may now need too much control to feel safe. Self-understanding asks: Where do I experience request as control? Where do I experience commitment as imprisonment? Where do I resist structure even when it would help me? Where do I need exit power? Where do I confuse guidance with domination?

The self needs agency, but agency is not the same as total control. A mature structure distinguishes healthy autonomy from reactive control, responsible commitment from coercive obligation, and supportive structure from dominating structure. The wound of powerlessness begins to heal when the self can choose structure without feeling enslaved by it.

11.4.2 Comparison and the Wound of Inferiority

Comparison can become wound when the self repeatedly experiences itself as less — less capable, less beautiful, less successful, less loved, less intelligent, less advanced, less worthy, less chosen, less visible. This wound often becomes active around peers, siblings, classmates, colleagues, social media, professional fields, romantic rivals, or public success. The person may feel: I am behind; I am smaller; I am not enough; their success reduces me; there is not enough space for me.

Comparison wound can produce envy, urgency, despair, contempt, or overachievement. The self may try to escape inferiority by becoming superior, but superiority does not heal inferiority — it often remains attached to the same comparison system. Self-understanding asks: Who do I compare myself to? What exactly do they seem to possess? What pain appears in comparison? What desire does comparison reveal? What standard am I using? Is this standard mine? What would growth look like outside this ranking system?

Comparison can provide information, but it should not define worth. The wound of inferiority requires the self to recover its own developmental path, not by denying difference or pretending comparison never matters, but by refusing to let ranking replace self-knowledge.

Both wounds of power and position involve a distorted relationship to relative standing. One responds by tightening control to avoid re-experiencing powerlessness; the other measures worth against others to escape inferiority. Healing requires finding agency without domination and worth without ranking — a relation to self that is not defined by position above or below others.

11.5 Mapping and Defensive Identity

To work with wounds constructively, the self needs tools for locating and understanding them. The sensitive zone provides a systematic framework for mapping what hurts, what triggers it, and what the self needs in response. From this mapping, it becomes possible to see how a wound can harden into a defensive identity — a version of the self organized around never being hurt the same way again.

A sensitive zone is an area of the self where wound, need, fear, and identity are tightly connected. When this zone is touched, the self reacts quickly. The sensitive zone may involve competence, desirability, intelligence, belonging, status, body, money, family background, education, accent, age, career, love, creativity, visibility, dependency, failure, or many other domains. The self should learn to map these zones using a systematic structure:

Sensitive Zone:
What area hurts easily?

Trigger:
What kind of event activates it?

Immediate Feeling:
What emotion appears?

Interpretation:
What meaning do I attach?

Defense:
How do I protect myself?

History:
What past injury may be connected?

Need:
What need is exposed?

Boundary:
What protection is required?

Agency:
What responsible action is possible?

For example, a person with a sensitive zone around competence might identify: the trigger is someone correcting their work; the immediate feeling is shame and defensiveness; the interpretation is that the other person thinks they are not good enough; the defense is to overexplain, justify, or withdraw; the history involves earlier humiliation around mistakes; the need is for respectful feedback that separates error from worth; the boundary is no contempt and specific criticism only; the agency lies in pausing, asking for concrete feedback, and revising without collapse. This map turns sensitivity into knowledge. It does not remove pain immediately, but it gives the self a way to work with pain.

A wound can also become identity. The self may organize around not being hurt again. A person wounded by dependence becomes “the independent one.” A person wounded by humiliation becomes “the competent one.” A person wounded by invisibility becomes “the exceptional one.” A person wounded by betrayal becomes “the skeptical one.” A person wounded by rejection becomes “the one who does not care.” A person wounded by powerlessness becomes “the one no one can control.”

This defensive identity may contain genuine strength — it may have helped the person survive. But it can become narrow. The defensive identity says: I will become the opposite of what hurt me. This can create development, but it can also keep the wound at the center of life. The person is not free if the whole identity is organized around avoiding an old pain. Self-understanding asks: What part of my identity was built to protect a wound? What strength did this defense create? What freedom did it cost? What would remain of me if I no longer had to organize around this injury?

This is delicate work. The defensive identity should not be attacked — it protected something. But it should be examined. A person may keep the strength without keeping the prison.

Mapping a sensitive zone transforms diffuse reactivity into structured self-knowledge. Recognizing a defensive identity reveals both the genuine protection it offered and the narrowing it demanded. Together, these tools allow the self to examine its protective structures with compassion and curiosity, keeping the strength while loosening the prison.

11.6 Wound Dynamics: Repetition, Overreaction, Boundary, and Agency

Wounds are not static marks — they generate dynamic patterns that play out across time, relationships, and situations. Understanding these dynamics is essential for moving from automatic reaction to conscious response. Four dynamics are particularly important for this work: repetition, overreaction, boundary, and agency.

11.6.1 Wound and Repetition

Wounds often repeat themselves. Not because the person consciously wants pain, but because the wound shapes expectation, attention, choice, and interpretation. A person wounded by unavailability may keep choosing unavailable people. A person wounded by criticism may keep entering fields where feedback feels like danger. A person wounded by invisibility may keep chasing recognition from people who cannot give it. A person wounded by control may keep interpreting all structure as domination. A person wounded by rejection may reject opportunities before they can reject them.

The wound creates a familiar world. Familiarity is not the same as safety, but the self may choose what it knows, even when what it knows hurts. Self-understanding asks: Where do I keep recreating the emotional shape of an old wound? What kind of person, field, task, or situation keeps activating the same pain? What do I expect before anything happens? How do my expectations shape what I choose?

Repetition is not destiny. But repetition must be seen before it can be interrupted.

11.6.2 Wound and Overreaction

Overreaction is often a sign that the present event is connected to a deeper structure. This does not mean the reaction is fake — it means the reaction has more than one source. There is the present event, the old wound, the meaning attached to the event, the defense that activates, the identity that feels threatened, and the body memory that joins the reaction.

The self should ask: What percentage of this reaction belongs to the present? What percentage may belong to the past? What meaning did I add? What old pain did this touch? What defense became active?

This question creates space. It allows the self to respond to the present without being completely governed by the past. A person can say: this hurt; the hurt is real; but the intensity may be older than the moment; I need to respond to both the present event and the old wound. This is mature self-understanding.

11.6.3 Wound and Boundary

Wounds need boundaries. A wound without boundary remains exposed. A wound with only rigid defense becomes isolated. A wound with conscious boundary becomes protected enough to heal and act. Boundaries around wound may include slower exposure, specific feedback rules, choosing safer people, refusing contempt, limiting contact with harmful environments, naming what is too much, asking for clarity, leaving situations that repeat injury, and protecting recovery time.

For example, a person with a humiliation wound around feedback may need the boundary: I can receive specific criticism of work, but I will not accept contempt or personal attack. A person with an abandonment wound may need: I need reliable communication, but I will not demand constant reassurance. A person with a betrayal wound may need: trust must be built through consistent behavior over time, and I will not grant full access immediately without evidence. A person with a control wound may need: I can accept commitment when exit, consent, and voice are preserved.

Boundary allows the self to remain open without being unprotected. It is the middle path between exposure and isolation.

11.6.4 Wound and Agency

A wound can reduce agency. When the wound is active, the self may feel trapped inside old reactions — it may hide, attack, please, freeze, overexplain, withdraw, control, collapse, or perform. The reaction feels automatic. Agency returns when the self can recognize: this is a wound response; I do not need to obey it immediately; I can pause; I can name what is happening; I can choose a boundary; I can ask for clarification; I can take a smaller action; I can separate past from present.

Agency around wound is usually gradual. At first, a person may only recognize the wound after reacting. Then they may recognize it soon after. Then while reacting. Then at the moment of activation. Then before entering situations likely to activate it. This progression matters:

after reaction
→ soon after reaction
→ during reaction
→ at activation
→ before activation
→ conscious response

The goal is not to erase sensitivity. The goal is to reduce automaticity and recover choice.

These four dynamics reveal how wounds operate as living patterns. Repetition and overreaction show how the wound unconsciously recreates its own conditions. Boundary and agency show how the self can interrupt those patterns — limiting exposure while recovering the capacity for choice. The progression from automatic reaction to deliberate response is the central movement of wound work.

11.7 Reading Wound Structurally and Final Integration

This chapter has examined wounds of visibility, relational safety, power, and position, along with the dynamics through which they operate and the tools for mapping them. To bring these threads together, a systematic question set can help the self read any wound with clarity and precision. This final section presents that structure and then offers the chapter’s culminating insight: the wound is real and shaping, but it is not the whole self.

To read wound structurally, use the following questions:

Wound:
What hurts repeatedly?

Trigger:
What kind of present event activates it?

Intensity:
Is the reaction larger than the event alone explains?

Original Injury:
What past humiliation, shame, rejection, abandonment, betrayal,
neglect, control, or invisibility may be connected?

Meaning:
What does the event seem to say about me?

Defense:
How do I protect myself?

Need:
What need is exposed?

Boundary:
What protection is required now?

Distortion:
What might the wound be making me misread?

Repetition:
Where does this wound recreate similar situations?

Agency:
What response is possible without denying the wound or
surrendering to it?

Revision:
What should be updated in my self-model?

This structure helps the self hold wound with clarity rather than being blindly governed by it. A wound is part of the self’s history and structure, but it is not the whole self. This distinction matters deeply. If the wound is denied, it continues operating invisibly. If the wound becomes total identity, the person becomes trapped inside injury.

The mature position is: this wound is real; it has shaped me; it affects my reactions; it deserves care and respect; but it is not all that I am; it does not own my future; it can be understood, bounded, and gradually revised through new evidence and action. A wound may create sensitivity, but sensitivity can become intelligence if structured. A person sensitive to humiliation may learn dignity and respectful feedback. A person sensitive to abandonment may learn reliability and attachment boundaries. A person sensitive to invisibility may learn the difference between recognition and performance. A person sensitive to control may learn consent, autonomy, and chosen commitment. A person sensitive to betrayal may learn discriminating trust.

The wound does not need to be worshiped. It needs to be understood.

This chapter has argued that a wound is not only something that happened — it is something that continues to organize present perception, reaction, and protection. The wounds examined — humiliation, shame, unmet recognition, rejection, abandonment, betrayal, powerlessness, and inferiority — each leave behind a distinct structure of sensitivity. But sensitivity, when understood, can become intelligence rather than automatic reaction. Through mapping sensitive zones, recognizing defensive identities, and understanding the dynamics of repetition, overreaction, boundary, and agency, the self can begin to read its wounds structurally. Wound and sensitivity show where the self has been injured and where the self still guards itself. To know oneself is to know these sensitive zones without becoming ruled by them. That is the work of this chapter.

  1. A wound is a past injury that continues to organize current perception, reaction, and protection — The event may be over, but the expectations, rules, and protective patterns it left behind remain active.
  2. Sensitivity is not weakness — it is a signal — A disproportionate reaction means the present event touched a deeper structure. Ask: “What percentage of this reaction belongs to the present, and what percentage to the past?”
  3. Three main types of wounds: visibility wounds (humiliation — fear of being seen; shame — fear of being known; unmet recognition — invisibility), relational safety wounds (rejection, abandonment, betrayal), power and position wounds (powerlessness, inferiority).
  4. The sensitive zone mapping framework: sensitive zone → trigger → immediate feeling → interpretation → defense → history → need → boundary → agency. This maps diffuse vulnerability into structural knowledge.
  5. Defensive identity: the self organizes around “never being hurt that way again” — becoming “the independent one,” “the strong one,” “the one who doesn’t care.” This identity contains real strength but can become a prison.
